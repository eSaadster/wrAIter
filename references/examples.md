# Before and After Examples

Read these as proofs. Each After keeps the concrete fact and cuts the performance around it. Length follows the thought. Some Afters stay compact because the claim is compact. Others run longer because the claim needs room.

## Example 1. The foil

Before. "This is not just another productivity tool. It is a complete rethink of how teams work."

After. "The tool puts tasks and chat in one window, so the team can stay inside one thread instead of chasing updates across apps."

Changes. The original spends a straw rival's credibility and then announces significance with no evidence. The rewrite says what the thing does and keeps the useful consequence in the same sentence.

## Example 2. The disguised foil

Before. "Shipping speed matters almost as much as code quality, and teams are investing more than ever."

After. "Teams now budget for release automation the way they budget for tests, because a broken release path costs them the same kind of time a missing test suite used to."

Changes. The original borrows weight from code quality and then from an invented rising past. The rewrite names the concrete behavior and keeps the reason attached. Note the analogy that survives. "The way they budget for tests" explains the new thing through a familiar one and demotes nothing, so the foil ban lets it through.

## Example 3. Comparative setup, short verdict

Before. "Some teams write documentation when time allows. The best teams write it before the code."

After. "Write the doc before the code, while the design is still easy to change and before a pull request hardens the wrong shape."

Changes. The "some teams" group existed to lose. Cut the group. The instruction stood alone from the start, and the rewrite keeps the practical why.

## Example 4. Throat clearing and emphasis crutch

Before. "Here is the thing about remote work. It is worth noting that most teams struggle with alignment. Let that sink in."

After. "Remote teams drift out of alignment without a written plan, and the drift shows up as duplicate work and missed handoffs."

Changes. Two announcements and one crutch gone. The claim arrives in the first sentence and carries a concrete cost with it.

## Example 5. Dramatic fragmentation

Before. "Speed. Quality. Cost. You can only pick two. That is it. That is the tradeoff."

After. "When speed, quality, and cost pull against each other, pick two and accept that the third will give."

Changes. One natural sentence, no performative emphasis. A real enumeration of three options is content, not a triplet. The ban targets decorative threes, and this list carries the actual constraint.

## Example 6. False agency

Before. "The decision emerges from the data, and the culture shifts toward quality."

After. "The tech lead reads the error rates and moves review earlier in the sprint, which changes how the team spends the week."

Changes. Decisions do not emerge. Someone decides. Cultures do not shift. Someone changes a habit. The rewrite gives both verbs a person and keeps the consequence in view.

## Example 7. Vague declarative, map and terrain

Before. "The implications of this outage are significant and the stakes are high."

After. "The outage cost four enterprise renewals and nine days of engineering time, and that is the bill the team is still paying."

Changes. "Significant" and "high stakes" pointed at the map. The rewrite walks the terrain and trades adjectives for numbers without chopping the thought short.

## Example 8. Rhetorical setup

Before. "What if I told you that the best teams do not optimize for productivity? Here is what I mean. They optimize for learning. Think about it."

After. "The best teams optimize for learning, and they treat each ship as a chance to see what the system taught them."

Changes. States the claim without the posturing, and drops productivity as a foil. The claim stands in an empty room and keeps a full sentence shape.

## Example 9. Over shortening

Before. "Remote work fails. Alignment dies. Plans save it."

After. "Remote work falls apart when nobody writes down the plan, because alignment has nowhere to live except in the shared doc."

Changes. The Before cut every sentence to three words and sounded like a slogan deck. The After keeps one clear cause and effect in ordinary speech. Crisp removed the filler. It did not remove the grammar.

## Example 10. Demonstration trap when cataloging catches

Before. "Structure matters too. 'Not X, it is Y' contrasts. Lists of what something is not before the reveal. Staccato fragments. Inanimate objects doing human verbs. 'The decision emerges' when someone decided."

After. "Structure matters too. State the point in one direction, lead with what something is, write full sentences at a natural length, and put whoever acts in the subject slot."

Changes. Positive instructions replace a fragment stack of quoted violations. No back to back bad examples. No false agency even in warning copy.

## Example 11. Demonstration trap in a closer

Before. "If your drafts sound polished but hollow, the problem might be the patterns, not the ideas. wraiter goes after the patterns so the ideas can breathe."

After. "Run your next draft through SKILL.md, review the edits, and keep any sentence that still needs its full length to say the thing clearly."

Changes. Cut the conditional setup, the binary contrast, and the metaphor. One instruction closes the piece without forcing a clipped cadence.
